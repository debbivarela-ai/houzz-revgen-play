The pro has already shown interest. Do not pitch. Diagnose immediately. Your goal is to confirm pain and earn a discovery call or close on the first call.

Open

"Hey [Name], this is [Your Name] from Houzz Pro — I saw you were just checking us out and wanted to connect quickly to make sure I point you in the right direction."

→ Keep it warm and low-pressure. They came to you — don't blow it by pitching immediately.

Diagnose

"Before I go into anything about Houzz Pro, I want to make sure what I show you is actually relevant to your business. Can I ask you a quick question?"

"What made you start looking into Houzz Pro today — was there something specific going on in your business that's driving this?"

→ Stop talking. Let them answer. This is the most important moment on the call.

"Tell me more about that — how long has that been a problem for you?"

→ Before advancing, you must get answers to all 5 qualifiers. These are not optional.

Discovery call — 5-stage structure

This call has one job: understand the pro's business deeply enough to show them exactly how Houzz Pro solves their specific pain. Do not demo features. Diagnose first.

Stage 1

"Before I show you anything, I want to make sure what I walk you through is actually built around how you work — not just a generic demo. So let me ask you a few questions first."

"Walk me through how you manage a project today — from the moment you get a lead, all the way through to getting paid. What does that look like?"

→ Listen fully. Don't interrupt. Take notes. If their answer is vague: "Can you give me a specific example from a project you finished recently?"

"What types of projects do you mostly work on — renovations, new builds, commercial, a mix?"

"How do you typically price your work — is it fixed price, cost-plus, or does it depend on the job?"

"How are you currently handling estimates? Are you using software, spreadsheets, or building them from scratch each time?"

"And when it comes to invoicing and getting paid — what does that process look like today?"

Stage 2

"You've given me a good picture of how things run. Now let me ask — where does it tend to break down? What's the part of your process that takes up the most time or causes the most headaches?"

→ If they dodge: "Think about a project from the last few months that didn't go as smoothly as you wanted — what got in the way?"

"Is that something that happens on most jobs, or just certain types?"

"What have you tried to do to fix it?"

Mandatory gate

→ These 5 questions are mandatory before advancing to stage 3. Every answer must be confirmed before you move forward.

"Before we go further — am I speaking with the owner, or is there someone else who would be involved in a decision like this?"

"If this turns out to be the right fit, how quickly are you looking to make a change? Are you thinking weeks, or is this more of a longer-term look?"

"Is sticking with your current process an option, or is this something you've decided you need to solve?"

"When it comes to a decision like this, is it just you, or does anyone else need to be part of the conversation?"

"Are you evaluating any other platforms right now, or is this your main focus?"

→ If status quo is acceptable, the decision maker is not present, or there is no timeline — stop and address this before proceeding.

Stage 3

"I want to make sure I understand the real cost of this — not just the frustration, but what it's actually doing to your business."

"When [their specific pain] happens — what does that cost you? Is it time, margin, client relationships, or all three?"

→ If they hesitate: "Even a rough estimate is fine — how many hours a week would you say this takes you?"

"And what does that mean for the rest of your business — are you turning down jobs, working nights and weekends, or losing track of where you stand financially on a project?"

"If you had to put a number on it — what do you think this is costing you per month, or per job?"

→ Let them quantify it. This is your anchor for the value conversation later.

Stage 4

"If we could solve [their specific pain] — what would that change for you? What would you do with that time or that margin?"

→ Let them articulate the value. Do not answer for them.

"What would it mean for your business if you could send a professional estimate in under an hour, get client approval digitally, and collect payment the same day the work is approved?"

"Based on everything you've shared, the two things I want to make sure you can do from day one in Houzz Pro are: send an estimate to a real client and accept an online payment. Those two things alone will change how your business runs. Let me show you exactly how."

→ This is the pivot to the demo. Everything you show must be tied directly to the pain they described.

Stage 5

"Based on what you've shared and what I've shown you — does this solve the problem you came in with?"

"What questions do you have before we talk about getting you set up?"

"The next step from here is to get you into Houzz Pro and get your first estimate and first online payment set up. That's the milestone that makes everything else click. Are you ready to move forward today, or is there something we need to address first?"

→ If they hesitate → use 3C. If they're ready → move directly into the pre-sale close script.

Manager coaching guide

Run the play with GROW coaching and SBI feedback. Don't rescue — develop.

Core principle: performance = potential − interference

Great managers do

  • Ask before telling
  • Tie feedback to observable behavior and impact
  • Reinforce the framework every single time
  • Drive rep ownership of outcomes

Red flags to avoid

  • Jumping in and solving the deal for the rep
  • Re-explaining the workflow instead of coaching it
  • Vague feedback ("that was good...")
  • Ignoring decision-making mistakes

SBI → GROW feedback sequence

Step 1: SBI — give clear, non-vague feedback first

S — Situation: "In the discovery call with that remodeler this morning…"

B — Behavior: "…you moved into the estimating demo before confirming how they currently price jobs."

I — Impact: "That meant the demo didn't connect to their actual pain — they disengaged after about 4 minutes."

Step 2: GROW — coach the path forward

G — Goal: "What were you trying to accomplish in that demo?"

R — Reality: "What did you actually learn about how they price before jumping in?"

O — Options: "What question could have confirmed their pricing model first?"

W — Way forward: "What will you ask on the next call before moving to any product feature?"

Objection handling — 3C framework

Connect → Clarify → Continue. Never rescue. Never discount first.

The 3C method

Connect

Acknowledge the concern and validate it from their point of view. Don't fight the objection.

Clarify

Ask a follow-up to understand the real concern beneath the stated one. Objections often mask 1 of 4 fears.

Continue

Provide the insight, story, or proof needed to address the root fear — then advance.

The 4 fears beneath objections: fear of being taken advantage of · fear of rejection · fear of change · fear of making a mistake.

Objection: "It's too expensive."

Connect

I hear that — budget is always a real consideration, and I don't want to waste your time with something that doesn't fit.

Continue

Pros who've switched from spreadsheets typically recover the cost in the first month from faster invoicing and fewer change-order disputes. What's one job from the last 6 months where margin slipped because of a process issue?

Objection: "I don't have time to learn new software."

Connect

Totally fair — most pros I talk to are already stretched thin. The last thing you need is another tool that takes 3 months to figure out.

Continue

Most pros are running live within a week, and the time savings show up in the first project — especially on estimates and client communication. What does your current estimate process look like?

Objection: "I already have a system that works."

Connect

Good — that's actually the best place to start. If something's already working, I don't want to fix what isn't broken.

Continue

When you say "works," is it fully integrated, or are there still pieces you're stitching together manually? Most pros have something, but there's usually one piece that still causes friction.

Objection: "I need to think it over."

Connect

Of course — this is a real decision and I want you to be confident in it.

Continue

Is there a specific part of what we talked about that you're still weighing? What would need to be true for this to be a clear yes?

Objection: "I want to check out some other options first."

Connect

That makes sense — you should feel good about what you're choosing.

Continue

What's most important to you as you evaluate? Most pros tell me it comes down to ease of use, invoicing speed, or client experience — is that the frame for you, or is there something else I should know?
